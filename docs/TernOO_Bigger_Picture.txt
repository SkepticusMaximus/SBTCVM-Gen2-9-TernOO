  WHY TernOO MATTERS BEYOND SBTCVM
  Ternary primitives, neural substrates, and a new
  consensus mechanism for distributed governance
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Author: Stevo (SkepticusMaximus), with synthesis assistance
        from Claude
Audience: Thomas Leathers and anyone else curious about
          where TernOO might lead
Date: April 2026


PURPOSE
-------

TernOO as a four-opcode extension to SBTCVM is, on its own,
a small experiment. This document explains why it might
become the seed of something significantly larger, by
walking through five connected ideas:

  1. What TernOO actually buys you at the machine level
  2. How a ternary substrate could underpin a new class
     of neural network hardware
  3. How that hardware could support a novel consensus
     mechanism for distributed governance
  4. Why a ternary machine might be the right place to
     revive object-uniform GUI design in the Smalltalk
     tradition
  5. What a small, transparent neural network running on
     ternary hardware could look like in practice

None of these are claims that TernOO must become any of
these things. They are observations about a coincidence
of structure that emerges when you take balanced ternary
seriously as a computational primitive.


PART ONE: TernOO AT THE MACHINE LEVEL
-------------------------------------

TernOO uses the leading trit of a 9-trit tryte as a type
tag. Three states, three meanings:

  T (-1)  EXECUTABLE
   0      DATA
  +1      REFERENCE

This costs one trit. In binary the equivalent cost would
be at minimum two bits, with one of the four combinations
either wasted or repurposed as an error case.

That difference sounds trivial. It is not.

Most distinctions in computing that cost two bits in binary
also cost one trit in ternary if we are willing to design
for it. Comparison results (less, equal, greater). Logic
with unknowns (false, unknown, true). Sign of a number
(negative, zero, positive). Voting outcomes (against,
abstain, for). State machine transitions (advance, hold,
retry). The list is long.

Each of these distinctions, when implemented in binary,
involves two-bit encodings and either software discipline
or hardware flag registers to manage the wasted state.
Each, when implemented in ternary with a TernOO-style type
trit, fits in one trit and dispatches in one CPU cycle.

The TernOO TYPEDISPATCH opcode is the simplest possible
demonstration of this: one opcode, three branches, one
cycle. On a ternary CPU it is structurally trivial. On a
binary CPU it requires a compare-and-branch-twice pattern,
or a flag register, or both.

The point is not that ternary is universally faster than
binary. The point is that when a problem is structurally
three-state — and many are — ternary expresses it with
zero overhead, while binary expresses it with constant
two-bit-encoding overhead at every operation.


PART TWO: TERNARY NEURAL HARDWARE
---------------------------------

Conventional CPUs are sequential abstraction layers over
transistors. They evolved this way because transistors
were once expensive and we needed to share them across
operations via clocking, registers, and instruction
decoding. That abstraction is general-purpose but expensive
for workloads that want massively parallel weighted-sum-
and-threshold operations — which is most of what neural
networks do.

GPUs and TPUs are partial admissions of this. They strip
away CPU complexity to get closer to the metal for tensor
math. They are still binary, however, because the hardware
underneath is still binary.

A three-state transistor — one with three stable conduction
levels rather than two — would map onto something neural
networks already need: signed weights. In binary, signed
weights require multiple wires or a sign bit. In balanced
ternary, the values -1, 0, and +1 are all directly
representable in a single signal. A neuron summing inputs
with -1/0/+1 weights costs one wire per connection instead
of two bits. At scale, this is a real density advantage,
not a marginal one.

There are research groups working on three-state-tolerant
analog circuits for exactly this reason. Ternary neural
networks are a recognised topic in machine learning
literature. The hardware is not yet mainstream, but the
mathematical and circuit-level groundwork exists.

Where TernOO fits in: SBTCVM is too slow and too small to
*run* a useful neural network. But it is well-suited to
*prototyping the encoding*. How do you represent a neuron
as a TernOO object? What does the type tag mean for
weights, activations, and gradients? What dispatch patterns
do training versus inference need? These are questions
that can be asked and answered on a 6.5 KHz ternary VM
even if the resulting code never trains a useful model.

The path from there to actual hardware is long, but it
exists. FPGAs can implement balanced ternary logic by
encoding each trit as two binary signals internally —
wasteful in transistor count but logically correct, and
sufficient to validate that a TernOO-style primitive set
makes sense for neural workloads. From validated FPGA
designs to ASICs is a known path. From ASICs to true
three-state-transistor silicon is the longest leg, and
the most speculative.

But the encoding work — figuring out what the right
ternary primitives are for neural computation — can begin
right now, on SBTCVM, with TernOO.


PART THREE: A NOVEL CONSENSUS MECHANISM
----------------------------------------

This part requires a brief detour into a separate project
of mine: the Constituent Governance Protocol (CGP), a
blockchain-anchored democratic governance system I have
been designing for some time.

CGP uses a three-token model — Citizen Coin, Agent Coin,
and Poli Coin — to represent three distinct kinds of
participation in a deliberative process. Citizens vote on
matters that affect them. Agents propose and amend.
Politicians ratify and execute. The three roles map onto
three token types because the roles are structurally
distinct and because three-way voting outcomes need a
three-state representation.

The consensus mechanism I have been thinking about is
called Proof of Deliberation. Instead of Proof of Work
(burn computation) or Proof of Stake (lock up capital),
Proof of Deliberation rewards meaningful participation in
the discussion that produces a decision. The forward pass
is the accumulation of votes toward ratification. The
backward pass is the propagation of legitimacy back to the
participants who contributed meaningfully to the outcome.

If those phrases sound familiar to anyone with a neural
network background, they should. Forward pass and backward
pass are the two phases of neural network training. Proof
of Deliberation is, structurally, a gradient descent
process where the loss function measures divergence from
collective deliberation and the weights being updated are
participants' standing in the network.

This is not a metaphor. It is the same mathematics. A
governance system that uses a neural-network-shaped
consensus mechanism is, in computational terms, training
a small neural network whose parameters are the
contributors' weights.

Now combine the three pieces:

  - A native ternary substrate where weighted-sum-and-
    threshold operations are cheap (the hardware piece)

  - A type-tag system that distinguishes participants,
    proposals, and votes natively (TernOO)

  - A consensus mechanism whose mathematics is neural-
    network-shaped from the ground up (Proof of
    Deliberation)

These three ideas fit together in a way that is not
obvious from any one of them in isolation. A ternary
neural substrate running TernOO-style primitives could
implement Proof of Deliberation more efficiently than any
binary equivalent, because:

  1. The weighted-sum operations are native to the hardware
  2. The three-way participant types (Citizen / Agent / Poli)
     fit naturally into the type-trit encoding
  3. The forward-and-backward propagation maps directly
     onto the network's own learning process

And there is a hardware-resistance advantage that mirrors
how Bitcoin resisted GPU and ASIC takeover for as long as
it could. Nobody currently builds three-state-transistor
neural inference hardware at scale. By the time anyone
did, the network would have years of training data
establishing the legitimate participants. The barrier to
gaming such a system is not "buy more compute" or "buy
more tokens" — it is "build novel hardware AND establish
historical contribution from before that hardware existed."
That is a different and arguably stronger moat than any
existing consensus mechanism offers.


WHAT THIS MIGHT MEAN FOR SBTCVM
-------------------------------

I am not asking SBTCVM to become any of this. I am
flagging that the work I am doing on TernOO is part of a
larger pattern of thinking, and that the SBTCVM Gen 4
plans you mentioned (24-trit architecture, protected mode,
CerberOS, SBTNET) sound to me like they could be part of
the same trajectory.

If a future Gen 4 includes type-tagged primitives natively,
and if SBTNET evolves into a real packet-switched ternary
network protocol, and if CerberOS provides protected
addressing for multi-tenant workloads, then the substrate
for ternary neural inference and ternary-native consensus
governance would exist as a single coherent platform.

That platform would be unique in computing. There is no
other ternary OS, no other ternary network, no other
ternary type-tagged ISA. A small community building one
together would be in a position to define a category of
machine that nobody else has even started.

I do not know if any of this resonates with where you
want to take SBTCVM. It might sound like overreach from
someone who has been working in ternary for two weeks and
just learned what bal27 is. That is fair. But I wanted to
write down the connection between TernOO and these larger
ideas, because the connection is real and because it
might inform whether Gen 4 design wants to anticipate
this kind of usage or stay deliberately scoped.

Either way, TernOO as it stands today on Gen2-9 is a small
working experiment that you are free to ignore. The fork
is not asking for upstream merge. The point of writing
this document is not to lobby for changes you do not want.
It is to make sure the larger picture is on the table for
when and if you ever decide it matters.


PART FOUR: NATIVE GUI ON A TERNARY MACHINE
-------------------------------------------

Modern GUI frameworks have accumulated a great many layers
of abstraction. Each layer is individually justified — the
layout engine, the rendering pipeline, the input router,
the accessibility tree, the theming system, the
internationalisation pass — but collectively they impose
a cost on programming that is as much cognitive as it is
computational. A text box in a modern GUI is not a single
object. It is a hundred objects spread across a dozen
subsystems, and to build with it you have to hold a model
of the whole stack in your head.

There is an older tradition in which GUIs are built as
uniform object systems from the bottom up. Smalltalk-80
is the canonical example. On the Xerox Alto — a machine
with a 5.88 MHz CPU and 128 KB of RAM, vastly less capable
than most modern microcontrollers — Smalltalk ran a
complete windowed GUI with text editing, graphics, and a
live debugger. It did this because every widget was just
an object, every interaction was just a message send, and
the entire system from the kernel up was uniform. No
abstraction tax. No leaky stack.

TernOO could be the foundation for a similar system on
future ternary hardware. Every widget becomes a TernOO
object. Every message send becomes a TYPEDISPATCH. The
"is this a method or a reference or a literal?" question
that Smalltalk had to answer in software becomes a single
hardware opcode (TYPECHK). A native ternary GUI
environment could be structurally simpler than a binary
Smalltalk while doing the same work.

The Smalltalk-80 virtual machine specification is
documented in the Blue Book (Goldberg and Robson, 1983),
which has been public for over forty years. Squeak, the
open-source Smalltalk descendant, is MIT-licensed. Neither
would be "ported" to TernOO directly — the bytecode
formats and memory layouts are too different. But the
Blue Book provides a proven template for what a minimal
object VM needs, and that template is small: a few
thousand lines of code for the core VM, with the image
(the running object database) added on top.

This is a long-horizon goal. It depends on Gen 4 being
fast enough to host a real GUI, and on CerberOS providing
framebuffer access and input events. Neither exists yet.
But if and when those arrive, a TernOO-native GUI
environment would be a genuinely novel thing: the first
graphical computing environment built on balanced ternary
primitives from the bottom up, with no inherited
assumptions from binary computing.


PART FIVE: NEURAL EXPERIMENTS ON TERNARY HARDWARE
--------------------------------------------------

Part two of this document argued that ternary substrates
could be good hosts for neural networks. Part five is
about what a ternary neural experiment could actually
look like in the near term, using hardware that exists
today or is on the Gen 4 horizon.

The direct reference point is a project called Attention-11
by Damian Buret — a single-layer single-head transformer
written in raw PDP-11 macro assembly, trained to reverse
eight-digit sequences. It runs on a DEC PDP-11/44 with
4 MB of RAM and a 6 MHz CPU. Dave Plummer demonstrated
it recently on his channel. It is a fully functional
transformer with attention mechanisms, fixed-point
arithmetic, forward and backward propagation, loss
calculation, and gradient descent, all implemented by
hand in assembly.

The PDP-11/44 is roughly one thousand times faster than
SBTCVM Gen2-9 but roughly ten thousand times slower than
a modern CPU. That gap shows that there is a huge range
of "useful neural network work" that is feasible on
hardware far slower than modern GPUs. Gen 2-9 is
probably too slow even for Attention-11-scale work, but
Gen 4 (especially if implemented in C or Rust rather than
Python) should be in the right performance bracket.

The correct near-term experiment is not to port an
existing neural network to TernOO. It is to design a
small transformer from scratch, sized for ternary
hardware, using Attention-11 as the architectural
template. The model would use ternary weights natively
(-1, 0, +1), which ternary neural network research has
already shown to be surprisingly effective in binary
implementations. On ternary hardware that advantage
compounds: the weights are not just small in value, they
are native to the instruction set.

A second, more speculative experiment: what does a
TernOO-native neural representation look like? If each
neuron is a TernOO object, each weight is a typed tryte,
and each activation is a TYPEDISPATCH on the neuron's
state, you get a neural network whose structure is
expressed in the same type system as the rest of the
program. That might be more than an aesthetic benefit.
Debugging, inspection, and interpretability all become
easier when the network's internal structure is visible
as ordinary objects instead of opaque tensors.

None of this competes with modern deep learning at scale.
The point is not to train GPT on a ternary machine. The
point is to use ternary hardware for a different kind of
work: small, interpretable, structurally transparent
models that do bounded, well-defined tasks. A ternary
Attention-11 running at Gen 4 speeds would be a
fascinating research instrument, not a production AI.

If parts four and five sound like separate threads,
consider this: a TernOO-native GUI environment with an
embedded small neural model could provide something no
modern system offers — a graphical development tool that
can suggest completions, recognise patterns, and assist
the programmer, all using a model that runs on the same
machine, in the same type system, in a few thousand
lines of transparent assembly. Not as a replacement for
human judgement but as a visible, debuggable helper.

This is a long way off. It may never happen. But the
shape of it is clear enough to write down, which is the
point of this document.


PRACTICAL NEXT STEPS (IF ANY OF THIS INTERESTS YOU)
----------------------------------------------------

If parts of this resonate, here are some concrete things
that could happen without committing to anything:

  - I continue building TernOO experiments on Gen2-9 as
    a testbed for the encoding patterns

  - You and I (and anyone else interested) discuss what
    type-tag primitives might look like in Gen 4 from the
    start, rather than retrofitted

  - Someone — possibly me, possibly someone with more
    neural network background — prototypes a small
    TernOO-based neural representation as a proof-of-

    the option of using a ternary substrate later if and
    when one becomes available

None of these block any of the others. None of them
require commitment from you beyond what you have already
generously given. They are just possibilities that exist
because TernOO exists.


CLOSING
-------

The reason I am writing this is that the connections
between these ideas only became clear to me recently,
during the work on TernOO. I had been thinking about CGP
for years, and ternary neural hardware as a vague
possibility for months. The native-GUI and embedded-neural
ideas are more recent, sparked partly by revisiting
Smalltalk-80's achievements on tiny hardware and partly
by watching Damian Buret's Attention-11 transformer run
on a PDP-11. TernOO was supposed to be a small weekend
experiment. Then I noticed that the type trit, the
neural weight encoding, the three-token governance model,
the object-uniform GUI tradition, and the transparent
neural models all shared the same underlying shape —
three-state distinctions that binary expresses awkwardly
and ternary expresses natively.

That shape, repeated across five different domains, feels
like it might be pointing at something. I do not know
what. I am writing it down so that other people can look
at it and tell me whether they see what I see, or whether
I am chasing a coincidence.

Either way, thank you for SBTCVM. Without it none of this
thinking would have had a place to land.

Stevo (SkepticusMaximus)
Adelaide, April 2026
