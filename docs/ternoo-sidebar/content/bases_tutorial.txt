═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  ABOUT THIS TUTORIAL
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Most working programmers are fluent in three counting
bases: decimal for human arithmetic, binary for
low-level work, and hexadecimal as a compact display
format for binary. Ternary is the odd one out — almost
nobody uses it in production because almost no hardware
supports it.

SBTCVM and TernOO change that locally. If you are
writing programs for a balanced ternary machine, you
need to think in ternary the way a binary programmer
thinks in bits. This tutorial is your bridge.

It has three parts:

  Lesson 1: Counting bases side by side
  Lesson 2: What each base is good at
  Lesson 3: Programming in ternary, especially with TernOO

Each lesson begins with learning objectives and ends
with worked examples. The tutorial concludes with a
self-test quiz.

Use the TernOO Object Inspector alongside this
tutorial. Many examples below are values you can punch
into the inspector to see them decoded in trits and
interpreted as TernOO typed trytes.


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  LESSON 1: THE FOUR BASES SIDE BY SIDE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

LEARNING OBJECTIVES
-------------------
After this lesson you should be able to:
  - Recognise the same number written in binary,
    decimal, hex, and balanced ternary
  - Convert small numbers between bases
  - Understand what each base costs and saves in
    terms of digits per value

CONTENT
-------

A counting base — also called a radix — is the number
of distinct digit values available in your number
system. Each digit position represents a power of the
base. The bases relevant to computing are:

  Base 2 (binary):     digits are {0, 1}
  Base 3 (ternary):    digits are {-, 0, +}  (balanced)
                       or {0, 1, 2}          (unbalanced)
  Base 10 (decimal):   digits are {0..9}
  Base 16 (hex):       digits are {0..9, A..F}

Balanced ternary is the variant SBTCVM uses. Its three
digits are -1, 0, and +1, often written as -, 0, +
or T, 0, 1.

A FIFTH BASE: SEPTEMVIGESIMAL (BALANCED BASE 27)
-----------------------------------------------

SBTCVM also has its own balanced base 27 (also called
"sept" for short, or just "bal27"). This is to balanced
ternary what hexadecimal is to binary: a compact display
format where every 3 trits collapse into a single base-27
character. A 9-trit nonet displays as exactly 3 characters,
a 6-trit tryte displays as 2.

Thomas's character set for SBTCVM bal27:

  Char | Value     Char | Value     Char | Value
  -----|------     -----|------     -----|------
   M   | -13        0   |   0        D   | +13
   N   | -12        1   |  +1        C   | +12
   P   | -11        2   |  +2        B   | +11
   Q   | -10        3   |  +3        A   | +10
   R   |  -9        4   |  +4        9   |  +9
   S   |  -8        5   |  +5        8   |  +8
   T   |  -7        6   |  +6        7   |  +7
   U   |  -6
   V   |  -5
   W   |  -4
   X   |  -3
   Y   |  -2
   Z   |  -1

Note that letters O and L are deliberately skipped
(too easy to confuse with 0 and 1). Negatives use
M through Z, where Z is closest to zero (-1) and M
is the furthest negative (-13).

The same example values from the table above, now in
five bases:

  Decimal | Binary    | Hex  | Bal Ternary | Bal27
  --------|-----------|------|-------------|------
       0  |        0  |   0  |         0   |    0
       1  |        1  |   1  |         +   |    1
      13  |     1101  |   D  |        ++-  |    D
      27  |    11011  |  1B  |       +000  |   10
      42  |   101010  |  2A  |      ++--0  |   2N
     100  |  1100100  |  64  |     ++0+0+  |   4S
     243  |  11110011 |  F3  |     +00000  |   90
     6561 | 1100110100001 | 19A1 | +00000000 |  900
     9841 | 10011001110001 | 2671 | ++++++++++ |  DDD

Notice that 27 (which is 27^1) is "10" in bal27, exactly
analogous to how 16 is "10" in hex and 10 is "10" in
decimal. The leading position carries one full base
unit. And 9841 — the maximum positive 9-trit value —
is just "DDD" in bal27. Three characters cover the
entire SBTCVM address space.

The conversion mathematics:
  1 bal27 digit  = 3 trits  = ~4.75 bits of info
  3 bal27 digits = 9 trits  = ~14.27 bits = 1 nonet
  2 bal27 digits = 6 trits  = ~9.51 bits  = 1 tryte

You'll see bal27 used by SBTCVM tooling: the romdump
utility's "-c" option produces a canonical hexdump-style
view using bal27 for both columns. It's the natural
display format for ternary memory contents.


THE SAME NUMBER IN MULTIPLE BASES
-----------------------------

  Decimal | Binary    | Hex  | Balanced Ternary
  --------|-----------|------|------------------
       0  |        0  |   0  |        0
       1  |        1  |   1  |        +
       2  |       10  |   2  |       +-
       3  |       11  |   3  |       +0
       4  |      100  |   4  |       ++
       5  |      101  |   5  |       +--
       6  |      110  |   6  |       +-0
       7  |      111  |   7  |       +-+
       8  |     1000  |   8  |       +0-
       9  |     1001  |   9  |       +00
      10  |     1010  |   A  |       +0+
      13  |     1101  |   D  |       ++-
      27  |    11011  |  1B  |      +000
      42  |   101010  |  2A  |     ++--0
     100  |  1100100  |  64  |    ++0+0+
     242  | 11110010  |  F2  |    +0000-
     243  | 11110011  |  F3  |    +00000

Notice that 243 (which is 3^5) has a particularly
clean balanced ternary representation: a single +
followed by five zeros. Powers of the base always
have this clean form. In binary, 256 (2^8) is similarly
clean: 100000000.

DIGITS PER VALUE
----------------

To represent the same range of values, different bases
need different numbers of digits. Information theory
tells us the minimum number of digits needed in base B
to represent N distinct values is log_B(N).

  Range 0..255 (256 values):
    Binary:  needs  8 digits  (2^8  = 256)
    Hex:     needs  2 digits  (16^2 = 256)
    Decimal: needs ~3 digits  (10^3 = 1000, overshoots)
    Ternary: needs  6 digits  (3^6  = 729, overshoots)

  Range -9841..+9841 (19,683 values, the SBTCVM range):
    Binary:  needs 15 digits
    Hex:     needs ~4 digits
    Decimal: needs  5 digits
    Ternary: needs  9 digits exactly (3^9 = 19,683)

Per digit, ternary is more compact than binary but
less compact than decimal or hex. One trit holds
about 1.585 bits of information, since log2(3) =
1.585. So a 9-trit number holds about 14.27 bits of
information, which is why it fits ~19,683 values.

WORKED EXAMPLE
--------------

Take the decimal number 42. Convert it to all four
bases by hand.

  Binary (divide by 2 repeatedly):
    42 / 2 = 21 r 0
    21 / 2 = 10 r 1
    10 / 2 = 5  r 0
     5 / 2 = 2  r 1
     2 / 2 = 1  r 0
     1 / 2 = 0  r 1
    Reading bottom-up: 101010

  Hex (divide by 16):
    42 / 16 = 2 r 10 (= A)
     2 / 16 = 0 r 2
    Reading bottom-up: 2A

  Balanced ternary (divide by 3, carry on remainder 2):
    42 / 3 = 14 r 0  -> write 0
    14 / 3 = 5  r 2  -> write -, carry +1 (so 5+1=6)
     6 / 3 = 2  r 0  -> write 0
     2 / 3 = 0  r 2  -> write -, carry +1 (so 0+1=1)
     1 / 3 = 0  r 1  -> write +
    Reading bottom-up: +-0-0

  Verification: +-0-0
    = 1*81 + (-1)*27 + 0*9 + (-1)*3 + 0*1
    = 81 - 27 + 0 - 3 + 0
    = 51... wait, that is wrong.

  Let me redo it more carefully:
    42 / 3 = 14 r 0
    14 / 3 = 4  r 2  -> write -, carry +1: quot = 5
     5 / 3 = 1  r 2  -> write -, carry +1: quot = 2
     2 / 3 = 0  r 2  -> write -, carry +1: quot = 1
     1 / 3 = 0  r 1  -> write +
    Reading bottom-up: +---0

  Verification: +---0
    = 1*81 + (-1)*27 + (-1)*9 + (-1)*3 + 0*1
    = 81 - 27 - 9 - 3 + 0
    = 42 ✓

  All four representations of 42:
    Binary:           101010
    Hex:              2A
    Decimal:          42
    Balanced ternary: +---0

Punch 42 into the TernOO Object Inspector. The "Trit
Layout" panel will show +---0 padded to 9 trits, the
"Decoded" panel will confirm the type trit is 0 (DATA),
and the assembly construction section will show how
to build it in TernOO assembler.


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  LESSON 2: WHAT EACH BASE IS GOOD AT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

LEARNING OBJECTIVES
-------------------
After this lesson you should be able to:
  - Identify which base is best suited to which task
  - Explain why ternary maps naturally to three-state
    distinctions
  - Recognise that no base is universally best

CONTENT
-------

Each base has things it does well and things it does
clumsily. Knowing which is which is part of being a
thoughtful programmer.

BINARY
------

Binary is best at:
  - Boolean logic. AND, OR, NOT, XOR have clean
    one-bit-per-value implementations.
  - Bitmasks and flag fields. Each bit is an
    independent boolean.
  - Hardware implementation. Two voltage levels are
    easy to distinguish reliably.
  - Powers of two. Doubling, halving, and addressing
    aligned memory all become bit shifts.

Binary is awkward at:
  - Three-state logic. You need two bits, leaving one
    of the four combinations as either redundant or
    an error case.
  - Negation. Twos-complement is clever but asymmetric:
    the most negative value has no positive equivalent.
  - Decimal display. Conversion is iterative and lossy
    for fractions like 0.1.

HEXADECIMAL
-----------

Hex is best at:
  - Compact display of binary data. Four bits per hex
    digit means a byte fits in two characters.
  - Memory addresses. They are usually displayed in
    hex because they line up with binary boundaries.
  - Color codes (#RRGGBB), checksums, hashes.

Hex is not really a different base for computation —
it is a notation for binary. Internally it is still
bits all the way down.

DECIMAL
-------

Decimal is best at:
  - Human-readable numbers. We grew up counting in it.
  - Financial arithmetic. Currency calculations need
    decimal-exact arithmetic to avoid rounding bugs.
  - Anything that interfaces with humans directly.

Decimal is awkward at:
  - Binary computation. Binary-coded decimal exists
    but is slow.
  - Compact storage. It needs about 3.32 bits per
    digit, which wastes space if encoded densely.

BALANCED TERNARY
----------------

Balanced ternary is best at:

  Symmetric integer ranges.
    The range -N..+N is exactly representable. There
    is no asymmetric edge case like binary twos-
    complement, where -128 has no +128 counterpart.

  Negation by trit-flip.
    To negate a number, flip every trit: every + becomes
    -, every - becomes +. No twos-complement, no
    borrow propagation. This is one of the most elegant
    properties of balanced ternary.

  Three-way comparison.
    Comparing two numbers has three possible outcomes:
    less, equal, or greater. Ternary maps each outcome
    to one trit value, allowing single-instruction
    three-way branches without flag registers.

  Three-state logic and tagging.
    Yes/no/maybe. Past/present/future. Buy/hold/sell.
    Code/data/reference. Each of these natural three-
    state distinctions costs exactly one trit. In binary
    they cost two bits with one combination wasted.

Balanced ternary is awkward at:

  Hardware implementation.
    Three reliable voltage levels are harder than two.
    Most physical computing systems chose binary for
    this reason, not because binary is mathematically
    superior.

  Bitwise operations on external data.
    When you receive bytes from a binary world, you
    have to convert. SBTCVM works internally in trytes
    and converts at the IO boundary.

  Floating point.
    There is no IEEE 754 equivalent for ternary. The
    field is open for someone to standardise it.

  Library and tooling ecosystem.
    Binary has 80 years of accumulated libraries.
    Ternary has SBTCVM, Setun, and a handful of
    research projects. The ecosystem gap is real.

THE NATURAL THREE-STATE MAPPING
-------------------------------

The single most useful thing about balanced ternary
for programming is that natural three-state distinctions
cost exactly one trit. Some examples:

  Comparison result:    less / equal / greater
  Logic with unknowns:  false / unknown / true
  Type tag (TernOO):    data / reference / executable
  Voting:               against / abstain / for
  Sign of a number:     negative / zero / positive
  Flow control:         skip / fall through / repeat

In binary, each of these requires two bits, and you
have one combination left over that has to be either
redundant (wasted) or an error condition. Over
millions of these distinctions in a real program,
the encoding overhead and the wasted-state bookkeeping
add up.

TernOO is essentially this insight applied at the
hardware level. The type tag is one trit because
there are exactly three kinds of thing it needs to
distinguish.

A NOTE ON KNUTH
---------------

Donald Knuth wrote about balanced ternary in volume 2
of The Art of Computer Programming. He called it
"perhaps the prettiest number system of all." He
discussed its arithmetic properties in detail and
noted that the symmetry of the number system makes
several algorithms more elegant than their binary
counterparts. If you want the rigorous mathematical
treatment, that is the place to look.


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  LESSON 3: PROGRAMMING IN TERNARY WITH TernOO
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

LEARNING OBJECTIVES
-------------------
After this lesson you should be able to:
  - Use the TernOO type trit appropriately for
    three-state distinctions
  - Recognise when ternary patterns simplify code
    that would be awkward in binary
  - Use the Object Inspector to design typed trytes

CONTENT
-------

TernOO uses the leading trit of a 9-trit tryte as a
type tag, leaving 8 trits for the payload. The three
type values are EXEC, DATA, and REF. This is the
natural three-state mapping from Lesson 2 made
concrete in hardware.

The Object Inspector tool decodes these typed trytes
interactively. The rest of this lesson uses examples
you can punch into the inspector to see them decoded.

PATTERN 1: TAGGED VALUES
------------------------

A function returning a result that might be a value,
a reference to another value, or an error code can
use the type trit to distinguish all three:

  type = DATA: payload is the result value
  type = REF:  payload is the address of the result
  type = EXEC: payload is the address of an error
               handler to invoke

In binary you would need a separate flag, a tagged
union, or a sentinel value. In TernOO it is one tryte.

Inspector example: try 42 (DATA 42), 6661 (REF to 100),
and -6061 (EXEC at 500) to see all three patterns.

PATTERN 2: THREE-WAY DISPATCH
-----------------------------

The TYPEDISPATCH opcode reads the type trit of reg1
and branches three ways in a single cycle:

  EXEC: jump to the address encoded in the trytes own
        payload (the tryte is self-describing as code)
  DATA: fall through to the next instruction
  REF:  jump to the address held in reg2

This is the heart of object-oriented dispatch in
TernOO. A method lookup that would take a vtable scan
and an indirect call in binary takes one opcode here.

PATTERN 3: SYMMETRIC ARITHMETIC
-------------------------------

Ternary negation is just trit-flip. The opcode
"invert1" replaces reg1 with -reg1 in one instruction,
with no two-complement carry propagation. This makes
routines like absolute value, sign extraction, and
symmetric range checks elegant.

  Sign of reg1 in one trit:
    Compare reg1 to zero.
    The result is -1, 0, or +1.
    That single trit IS the sign — no extraction
    needed.

PATTERN 4: COMPARISON WITHOUT FLAGS
-----------------------------------

Binary CPUs have a flag register that holds the result
of the most recent comparison, then conditional jumps
test those flags. SBTCVM does it differently:

  gotoifless    jump if reg1 < reg2
  gotoif        jump if reg1 == reg2
  gotoifmore    jump if reg1 > reg2

Three jumps, three outcomes, no flag register, no
two-step compare-then-branch. The comparison happens
inside the jump opcode itself.

PATTERN 5: STATE MACHINES
-------------------------

A state machine where each state has up to three
possible transitions becomes very clean in TernOO.
The current state is a tryte. Reading its type trit
tells you which of three transition handlers to
invoke. The TYPEDISPATCH opcode does the routing in
one cycle.

A FRACTAL ASIDE
---------------

The classic fractal program Fractint, developed by
the Stone Soup Group in the late 1980s, used integer
arithmetic to render the Mandelbrot set quickly on
period hardware. Each pixel is iterated until either
the value diverges or a maximum iteration count is
reached. Each iteration produces one of three
outcomes: still iterating, converged, or diverged.

On a TernOO system the divergence flag fits in one
trit. The iteration counter is a small integer that
a 9-trit tryte holds easily. A complex number could
be represented as a small TernOO object with real and
imaginary parts as DATA fields and a divergence state
as a third field. The three-way branch on divergence
state would be a single TYPEDISPATCH opcode per pixel.

SBTCVM is too slow and too memory-limited to render
serious fractals in any reasonable time. But the
encoding pattern is interesting because it shows how
three-state outcomes that are awkward in binary fit
naturally into a ternary representation. Whether or
not anyone ports Fractint to SBTCVM, the approach is
a useful illustration of the ternary advantage.

PROGRAMMING ATTITUDE
--------------------

When you write programs for SBTCVM with TernOO, look
for places where the three-state mapping fits the
problem. Do not force three states where two are
correct. Do not avoid three states where they exist
naturally. Most ordinary computing — counting,
arithmetic, control flow without branching — looks
much the same in ternary as in binary, just with
different digit symbols. The interesting moments are
the ones where the ternary structure expresses
something the binary structure obscures.

Those moments are where TernOO earns its keep.

  SELF-TEST QUIZ
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Answer each question. Answers are after a spoiler gap.

1. How many distinct values can a single trit hold,
   and what are they in balanced ternary notation?

2. Approximately how many bits of information does
   one trit carry?

3. How many trits are needed to represent the SBTCVM
   address range -9841..+9841?

4. Convert the decimal number 13 to balanced ternary
   by hand.

5. In balanced ternary, how do you negate a number?

6. Why does ternary make three-state distinctions
   more efficient than binary?

7. In TernOO, what are the three type trit values
   and what does each one mean?

8. Which TernOO opcode performs three-way branching
   in a single cycle?

9. Name three distinctions in everyday programming
   that have exactly three states.

10. Why is binary still dominant in physical computing
    despite ternary having some mathematical advantages?





  - - - - - - - - - - SPOILER GAP - - - - - - - - - -





ANSWERS
-------

1. Three values: -, 0, + (or T, 0, 1, depending on
   notation).

2. About 1.585 bits, since log2(3) is approximately
   1.585.

3. Nine trits, since 3^9 is exactly 19,683.

4. 13 in balanced ternary:
     13 / 3 = 4 r 1   write +
      4 / 3 = 1 r 1   write +
      1 / 3 = 0 r 1   write +
     Reading bottom-up: +++
     Verification: 1*9 + 1*3 + 1*1 = 13 ✓

5. Flip every trit: each + becomes -, each - becomes +,
   and 0 stays 0. No twos-complement, no carry
   propagation, no asymmetric edge case.

6. A natural three-state distinction fits in one trit
   exactly. In binary it requires two bits, leaving
   one of the four combinations either wasted or
   reserved for an error case.

7. EXEC (-1) means executable code; DATA (0) means
   a literal value; REF (+1) means a reference to
   another memory location.

8. TYPEDISPATCH (opcode 201) reads the type trit of
   reg1 and branches three ways based on its value.

9. Acceptable answers include: less/equal/greater,
   yes/no/unknown, code/data/reference, negative/zero/
   positive, past/present/future, true/false/null,
   buy/hold/sell, against/abstain/for.

10. Hardware reasons. Two reliable voltage levels are
    easier to engineer than three. Binary won the
    physical computing race for engineering reasons,
    not mathematical ones. The Soviet Setun computer
    in the late 1950s showed that ternary hardware was
    feasible, but the binary ecosystem had already
    achieved critical mass.
